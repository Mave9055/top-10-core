## SESSION 06: THE BROKEN MIRROR

**Time:** 75 minutes  
**Law:** You were looking in a broken mirror.  
**Tool:** *"That's not the truth. That's the broken mirror talking."*

---

### [0:00 – 8:00] THE ANCHOR

Listen up.

Yellow light. Red light. You run this room. If you need to leave, you leave. If you need to stand, you stand. You are in charge.

Feet flat on the floor. Hand on chest. Hand on belly. Breathe in for four. Hold. Out for six. Do it again. Good.

Last session: Law 5 – Work becomes the war you can win.

Today: Law 6. Here it is:

**You were looking in a broken mirror.**

Your first attachment – usually to your mother – is the mirror where you learn who you are. If that mirror is cracked, distorted, or absent, you learn: *I am not enough. I am unlovable. My existence is a problem.*

That's not the truth. That's the damage. But it lives in your body like a fact.

Let's see how that works.

---

### [8:00 – 48:00] THE EPISODE (Expanded)

We're going to read about the first mirror – and how it cracked before he had words for it.

(Open the book. Read from Chapter 1, page 3-4 – the NICU and the glass wall.)

> *"Three pounds, four ounces of undercooked soul dropped into a box of glass and wires. I spent my first thirty days in an incubator – no mother's arms, no skin-to-skin, just machines and a sterile kind of silence. My body learned distance before my mind even knew the word."*

> *"Some people grow up learning how to trust the world. I grew up learning how to brace for it. I don't have memories from those days – but my body does. The tightness in my chest when someone gets too close. The way my skin hums under fluorescent lights. The feeling that I've always been separate, like I'm looking at life through a glass wall."*

That's the first mirror. Not a reflection saying "you're loved." A glass wall saying "you're alone." A newborn's brain is supposed to learn safety through touch, smell, skin. He got wires and alarms. **Distance became his first language.**

Now here's how that blueprint shaped everything after.

(Read from Chapter 10, page 22 – VLBW vulnerabilities.)

> *"Research suggests VLBW folks face higher risks: anxiety, depression, attachment issues, sensory hypersensitivity, altered stress response. My life has been a highlight reel of trauma hitting these exact vulnerabilities."*

But here's the part that matters for Law 6. The attachment wound.

(Read from Chapter 11, page 24 – echoes and amplifiers, attachment.)

> *"Relationships became the ultimate collision point. The blueprint suggested potential for insecure attachment, a deep-seated fear of abandonment. This wasn't abstract; it was a raw nerve."*

That raw nerve – fear of abandonment – isn't abstract. It's a black hole in the chest that never goes away.

Now read this. (From Chapter 4 reflection, page 15 – the black hole.)

> *"The Architect had a 'black hole' sensation in his chest that never went away. He felt physically invisible when he wasn't performing for others. He'd say, 'I'm just a people-pleaser' or 'I don't know who I am.' He felt unlovable. He felt 'worthless.'"*

**"A black hole sensation in his chest that never went away."** That's the mother wound. Not sadness. Not loneliness. A void. A place where self should be, but instead there's just absence. Because the mirror never showed him a self worth reflecting.

Now let's look at how the broken mirror created the fawn response – the people-pleasing that erases who you are.

(Read from Chapter 4 reflection, page 15 – erasing self.)

> *"My fawn response, perfected early, convinced me that erasing myself was the only way to keep any kind of peace. I distrusted my own perceptions... I ended up believing I was the monster in every room."*

**"Erasing myself was the only way to keep any kind of peace."** That's what the broken mirror teaches you: Your presence is a problem. The solution is to disappear. Not physically – though he hid in closets – but emotionally. Stop having needs. Stop having opinions. Stop taking up space.

Now here's where the broken mirror meets the world of labels and misdiagnosis.

(Read from Chapter 4, page 11-12 – BPD misdiagnosis.)

> *"I started reading about Borderline Personality Disorder. On paper, it fit too well: emotional chaos, relationship wreckage, shame, fear of abandonment. I self-diagnosed as borderline and carried that like a guilty verdict for over a year."*

> *"Then a therapist said, 'You don't have BPD. You have Complex PTSD. And you've been manipulated.' One sentence, and the floor shifted. Under all that mess, I didn't have a broken personality. I had a trauma injury."*

He spent a year calling himself borderline. Because the broken mirror said: *You're the problem.* He believed it. That's what the mother wound does. It makes you diagnose yourself as defective so you never look at who broke the mirror in the first place.

Now listen to what the broken mirror did to his sense of self.

(Read from Chapter 4, page 10 – the verdict he internalized.)

> *"Then came the verdict: I was selfish. I 'only thought about' myself. You hear that long enough, it sinks into your bones."*

His pain got flipped. Every time he tried to express what he was feeling, it got turned back on him. "You think you're suffering? Try being me." "You're obsessing and making something that's not there." So he stopped trusting his own perceptions. He stopped believing his own pain. That's the broken mirror.

Now let's go back to the letter he wrote to his six-year-old self. This is the repair.

(Read from Chapter 14, page 32 – letter to younger self.)

> *"I see you under that blanket. Hearing Mom scream. Heart hammering. It's not your fault. You're just a kid in a terrifying storm."*

> *"Back then, you learned to survive: Fear is too big. You're to blame. Stay small and silent. This will never end. Those thoughts made sense for a scared kid in danger. They aren't truth."*

> *"That terror wired you for fear, from the NICU to those nights of violence. It's why you feel everything so deeply. But that wiring doesn't define you. You aren't weak for freezing. You're tough as hell for making it through."*

> *"You'll carry this pain for a while. But you'll learn to set it down. You'll find ways to feel fear without hiding, to love without bracing for loss. Keep going. You've got a heart that won't quit. I'm proud of you for surviving."*

That's the antidote. The adult writing to the child. Not shaming him. Not telling him to get over it. Just sitting with him. Saying: *It wasn't your fault. You did what you had to do. I'm proud of you.*

Now read this from the 60 Reflections.

(Open the book. Read from Chapter 12, page 26 – the mirror lies.)

> *"The mirror lies when shame's in the room. It shows you a monster instead of a mechanic."*

That's Law 6 in one sentence. **The mirror lies.** The broken mirror shows you a monster – something wrong with your soul. But you're not a monster. You're a mechanic who never got a manual. The broken mirror isn't the truth. It's just the distortion you inherited.

Now read the last piece – the shift that happened when he got the right diagnosis.

(Read from Chapter 9, page 12-13 – the therapist's two minutes.)

> *"She explained that C-PTSD gets mistaken for BPD all the time. From the outside, trauma-wired behavior looks like instability, like 'personality problems.' People see the outbursts, the shutdowns, the panic, the relationship chaos. They don't see the blueprint underneath – the NICU, the violence, the losses, the twenty years of static. Under all that mess, she said, I didn't have a broken personality. I had a trauma injury."*

That's the shift. Not a broken personality. A trauma injury. One is identity. One is history. For years, he thought his identity was "broken." The therapist gave him a different frame: injured. And injuries can heal. Not instantly. Not completely. But they're not the whole of who you are. **You are not your wound. You are the person who carries it.**

### [48:00 – 58:00] THE MECHANISM (Whiteboard Drawing)

(Walk to whiteboard. Draw large, simple arrows.)

Here's how the broken mirror gets installed. Watch.

**Step 1:** Draw a box. Label it **"FIRST ATTACHMENT (MOTHER / CAREGIVER)"**

This is where you learn who you are. A baby doesn't have a self yet. The self is built through the mother's face, her touch, her voice. If she's present and loving, you learn: *I matter.*

**Step 2:** Draw an arrow to a second box. Label it **"MIRROR IS CRACKED / ABSENT"**

But if she's traumatized, depressed, addicted, absent, or violent – the mirror is broken. She can't show you that you matter because she never learned it herself.

**Step 3:** Draw an arrow to a third box. Label it **"CHILD LEARNS: 'I AM UNLOVABLE' "**

The child doesn't think "Mom has a problem." The child thinks: *The problem is me. I'm too much. I'm not enough. If I were better, she'd love me.*

**Step 4:** Draw an arrow to a fourth box. Label it **"FAWN RESPONSE / PEOPLE-PLEASING"**

So you learn to perform. To earn love. To manage her emotions. To disappear when you're inconvenient. You become whoever she needs you to be, because being real got you nothing.

**Step 5:** Draw an arrow to a fifth box. Label it **"BLACK HOLE / NO CORE SELF"**

Inside, there's a void. No solid "me." Just a chameleon changing colors to survive. You feel empty. You look for someone – anyone – to fill the hole. But no one can, because the hole was carved before you had words for it.

**Step 6:** Draw a loop arrow from the black hole box back to "FAWN RESPONSE"

Every relationship that fails confirms the original lesson. Every time someone leaves, you think: *See? I am unlovable.* The loop keeps the broken mirror in place for decades.

Point to the loop and say:

This is the mother wound. It's not about blaming her. She was probably looking into her own broken mirror. But the damage is real. **You didn't come out broken. You came out looking into a mirror that couldn't show you the truth.**

### [63:00 – 70:00] THE MIRROR (Silent – No talking)

You don't have to say a word. Just sit. Breathe. And notice.

If you've ever felt like you have to be perfect to be loved.

If you've ever felt like you disappear when you're not helping someone else.

If you've ever felt like a fraud waiting to be found out.

If you've ever looked in a mirror and didn't recognize who was looking back.

If you've ever apologized for existing.

If you've ever felt that black hole in your chest – the emptiness that never quite fills.

If you've ever been told you're "too sensitive," "too much," "too needy."

If you've ever believed that your needs are a burden.

If you've ever thought, "If people really knew me, they'd leave."

That's not proof you're broken. That's the echo of a broken mirror.

That's the mirror. That's the machine showing you where the crack started.

### [70:00 – 75:00] THE SHIFT + CLIFFHANGER

Here's your tool for this week. One thing.

When that black hole opens up – when you feel unlovable, invisible, like you have to perform just to deserve to exist – don't try to fill it. Don't earn it. Don't fix it.

Just name it. Say it out loud or in your head:

**"That's not the truth. That's the broken mirror talking."**

That's it. Naming it gives your brain one second of air. One second to separate who you are from what you were shown.
