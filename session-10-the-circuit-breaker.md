**You leave your body. The Circuit Breaker.**

You know the feeling. A fight happens, and suddenly you're watching from the ceiling. Someone's yelling, but it sounds far away, like through water. You're driving, and you realize you don't remember the last ten miles. You feel like a ghost. Like you're not real. Like the world is made of glass.

That's not "spacing out." That's not laziness. That's **dissociation** – a circuit breaker that trips when the pain is too much to carry. Your nervous system disconnects your mind from your body so you don't shatter completely.

Let's see how that works.

### [8:00 – 48:00] THE EPISODE (Expanded)

We're going to read about the first time he felt like he was watching life through a glass wall – before he had words for it.

(Open the book. Read from Chapter 1, page 3-4 – the glass wall.)

> *"Three pounds, four ounces of undercooked soul dropped into a box of glass and wires. I spent my first thirty days in an incubator – no mother's arms, no skin-to-skin, just machines and a sterile kind of silence. My body learned distance before my mind even knew the word."*

> *"The feeling that I've always been separate, like I'm looking at life through a glass wall."*

That's dissociation. Before he had words for it. The NICU taught him that connection happens through barriers. That you can be present but not really there. **The glass wall isn't a metaphor. It's his first memory – not of memory, but of feeling separate.**

Now let's look at how dissociation showed up in his sleep – the body screaming while the mind stayed silent.

(Read from Interlude, page 5 – sleep paralysis and screams.)

> *"Sleep paralysis is when your brain wakes up before your body does. For people with complex trauma, this hits harder. Childhood trauma wires the nervous system for hypervigilance. The threat detector stays set to sensitive, always ready to blare. Sleep gets broken, and the body never fully relaxes."*

> *"As I grew older, paralysis gave way to something worse: the screams. Not conscious cries, but sounds ripped from somewhere deeper, primal alarms tearing through the night. I'd wake to shaken shoulders, to voices telling me I had been screaming bloody murder, but I remembered nothing."*

Let that land. His body was screaming. His mind had no memory of it. That's not madness. That's a nervous system so locked in survival mode that it bypassed consciousness entirely. The circuit breaker tripped so hard that the experience didn't even get recorded. **The body remembered everything. The mind slept through it.**

Now read this from the 60 Reflections.

(Read from Chapter 12, page 26 – dissociation lines.)

> *"You don't lose your mind. You lose your baseline."*

> *"Dissociation isn't a choice. It's a circuit breaker."*

> *"The body doesn't forget. It archives. Therapy just hands you the filing system."*

That's Law 10. Not a choice. A protection. Your system disconnects so you don't shatter. The circuit breaker trips when the current is too high. Same principle. Same survival logic.

Now let's look at how dissociation connects to the rescue boat – the desperate attempt to turn down the volume.

(Read from Chapter 12.5, page 27-28 – the alarm and the boat.)

> *"When that alarm won't shut off, you reach for anything that dials it down long enough to breathe and stay alive another night. That's the rescue boat."*

Dissociation is the body's internal rescue boat. When the alarm is too loud, when the pain is too much, when you can't fight, can't flee, can't freeze, can't fawn – the circuit breaker trips. You leave. Not physically. But mentally, emotionally, sometimes completely.

Now read this about the cost of dissociation – the long-term effects.

(Read from Chapter 12.5, page 28 – the cost.)

> *"The stigma, the gaslighting, and the stoic 'suck it up' culture – whether military or civilian – made everything worse. It told me vulnerability was weakness, asking for help was failure, and my survival strategies proved I was broken. So I hid. I isolated. I didn't show up."*

Dissociation is a form of hiding. Not hiding in a closet – hiding inside your own head. You're still there. You're still breathing. But you're not present. And the shame makes it worse. The shame says: You're lazy. You're checked out. You don't care.

But that's not true. You left because staying was unbearable.

Now let's look at how dissociation appears in the legal system – memory gaps and confusion.

(Read from Chapter 18, page 37 – dissociation and memory gaps.)

> *"Dissociation & Memory Gaps: Stress-induced detachment meant I didn't fully encode instructions from judges or probation officers. Later, I genuinely couldn't recall specifics. It wasn't intentional forgetting. It was a brain protecting itself by not recording overwhelming input."*

That's crucial. You're not lying when you say you don't remember. Your brain literally didn't record the information. The circuit breaker tripped, and the memory never got written. That's not manipulation. That's survival.

Now read the 60 Reflections line about losing baseline.

(Read from Chapter 12, page 26 – again, for emphasis.)

> *"I didn't lose my mind. I lost my baseline."*

That's the best description of dissociation you'll ever get. You're not crazy. You're not losing your mind. You're just operating from a different baseline – one where connection feels dangerous, where presence feels like exposure, where leaving your body feels like the only safe option.

One more piece. This is from Chapter 10 – the blueprint and dissociation.

(Read from Chapter 10, page 22 – shaky foundation.)

> *"Think of being born VLBW in that 80s NICU as starting life on shaky ground. When the big storms hit – trauma, loss, toxic relationships – they rocked my foundation harder because it wasn't solid to begin with."*

Dissociation is the body's way of creating shaky ground on purpose. If the foundation is already cracked, the circuit breaker is more likely to trip. You're not weak. You're working with a system that was built in a hurricane.

Now read the closing line from Chapter 12.5 – the ember and the fight.

(Read from Chapter 12.5, page 29 – the ember.)

> *"The storm wired us for danger. The fire taught us to burn. But the rescue boat? It was never the enemy. It was proof we still wanted to live."*

Dissociation is a rescue boat. It's not the enemy. It's proof your system is still fighting. It's proof you found a way to survive when the alternative was complete collapse.

### [48:00 – 58:00] THE MECHANISM (Whiteboard Drawing)

(Walk to whiteboard. Draw large, simple arrows.)

Here's how the dissociation circuit breaker works. Watch.

**Step 1:** Draw a box. Label it **"OVERWHELMING PAIN / TERROR"**

Pain that exceeds what your nervous system can hold. Violence. Loss. Medical trauma. Betrayal. The system starts to overload.

**Step 2:** Draw an arrow to a second box. Label it **"FIGHT / FLIGHT / FREEZE FAIL"**

None of the usual responses stop the pain. You can't fight it. Can't run. Can't even freeze effectively. The pain is still there. The alarm is still blaring.

**Step 3:** Draw an arrow to a third box. Label it **"CIRCUIT BREAKER TRIPS (DISSOCIATION)"**

Your nervous system throws a switch. It disconnects consciousness from the body. You're not "gone" – but you're not fully here. The pain becomes distant. Like it's happening to someone else. Like you're watching a movie of your own life.

**Step 4:** Draw an arrow to a fourth box. Label it **"YOU SURVIVE THE UNBEARABLE"**

The dissociation works. You make it through. The immediate threat passes. Your system notes: *Disconnection = survival. Do it again.*

**Step 5:** Draw an arrow to a fifth box. Label it **"LONG-TERM COSTS"**

But the cost is high. You feel unreal. You have memory gaps. You lose time. You struggle to feel present in your own life. You feel like a ghost. You feel like you're watching from behind glass.

**Step 6:** Draw a loop arrow from the costs box back to "OVERWHELMING PAIN / TERROR"

Now dissociation becomes the default for any stress, not just catastrophe. A loud noise. A conflict at work. A crowded store. The circuit breaker trips when it doesn't need to. **You're not spacing out. You're surviving. But the survival program is running on a world that no longer requires it.**

Point to the loop and say:

This is dissociation. It's not a character flaw. It's a circuit breaker that protected a child from shattering. The problem isn't that it's broken. The problem is that it kept running after the threat was gone. **You're not crazy. You're just running an emergency program in peacetime.**
