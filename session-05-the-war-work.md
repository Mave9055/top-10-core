## SESSION 05: THE WAR-WORK

**Time:** 75 minutes  
**Law:** Work becomes the war you can win.  
**Tool:** *"This isn't discipline. This is panic wearing a suit."*

---

### [0:00 – 8:00] THE ANCHOR

Listen up.

Yellow light. Red light. You run this room. If you need to leave, you leave. If you need to stand, you stand. You are in charge.

Feet flat on the floor. Hand on chest. Hand on belly. Breathe in for four. Hold. Out for six. Do it again. Good.

Last session: Law 4 – What you couldn't mourn haunts you.

Today: Law 5. Here it is:

**Work becomes the war you can win.**

You're the first one in and the last one out. You never say no. You work through lunch, through sickness, through exhaustion. They call you a hero. They call you dedicated. They give you raises and praise.

But you're falling apart. You're running on fumes. And the moment you stop, the noise comes back. The grief. The shame. The alarm.

That's not dedication. That's a nervous system trying to find control in the only place it can. Work became your war because the other wars – the ones inside – felt impossible to win.

Let's see how that works.

---

### [8:00 – 48:00] THE EPISODE (Expanded)

We're going to read about a man who tried to outrun his own nervous system by going north.

(Open the book. Read from Chapter 7, page 18.)

> *"When Trinity took the kids, the house emptied out in a way that felt physical. Four bedrooms reduced to echoes. I couldn't afford it alone. Didn't need the space anyway. I slept on a buddy's floor for a while, my life compressed down to whatever could fit beside a mattress and a pipe. I needed distance. A reset. Somewhere far enough away that the noise might finally stop."*

> *"So I went north. Williston, North Dakota. Full-time work at Love's Truck Stop. Cold air. Long hours. Structure. I told myself it was a fresh start. What I didn't understand yet was that you can't outrun a nervous system. You just give it new terrain."*

That's the trap. You pack a bag, drive a thousand miles, and think the fire will stay behind. It doesn't. **You just give it new terrain.** And in the new terrain, the fire takes a different shape.

Now listen to what that shape looked like.

(Read from Chapter 7, page 18-19.)

> *"Work became the next addiction. It was cleaner. Legal. Praised. I threw myself into it with the same compulsive intensity I'd always used to survive. If I stayed busy – stocking, cleaning, hauling, closing – maybe I wouldn't feel the weight of my mother's death in December 2021. Maybe I wouldn't think about the divorce. Maybe I wouldn't feel the ache of my kids being gone."*

> *"For the first six months, they thought I was a miracle. I worked like five people. Learned every position. Took every extra task. Unloaded trucks – dry goods, walk-in, deep freeze. Stocked everything. Cleaned what no one else wanted to touch. I trained new hires not because I was promoted, but because I'd absorbed everything through sheer, compulsive effort."*

Notice the language. **"Compulsive effort." "Addiction."** He's not describing dedication. He's describing a nervous system running from pain by running on a treadmill that never stops. And everyone around him is clapping.

But here's what "compulsive effort" looks like from the inside.

(Read from Chapter 7, page 19 – the burnout.)

> *"Burnout doesn't announce itself. It creeps in quietly. One morning, I was scheduled for a 5 a.m. shift. I woke to pounding on my apartment window. Sunlight everywhere. It was 9 a.m. Shame hit like a gut punch. I swore it wouldn't happen again. The next morning, it did. Same pounding. Same time. Same humiliation."*

He's not lazy. He's exhausted. His body is shutting down because it's been running on fumes for months. But the shame doesn't say "rest." The shame says "you failed." So he pushes harder.

Now let's look at the workload that broke him.

(Read from Chapter 7, page 19 – impossible workload.)

> *"We were perpetually short-staffed. For four solid months, I closed both restaurants alone. Every dish from two kitchens. Sweeping and mopping massive floors. Hauling trash. Cleaning three deep fryers. Boxing leftover food. Doing prep for the next day. Sometimes it was two in the morning before I left, reeking of grease and exhaustion."*

> *"Even then, boundaries didn't exist. The night manager from the store side – someone I didn't even technically work for – would wander back, see me drowning, and ask me to do extra tasks for him. Coffee. Cleaning. Whatever he didn't want to deal with. I couldn't say no."*

**"I couldn't say no."** That's Law 7 – the fawn response – fused to work. You can't set boundaries because setting boundaries means risking disapproval. And disapproval feels like survival threat. So you say yes. Every time. Until you break.

Now listen to the isolation that came with it.

(Read from Chapter 7, page 19-20 – social anxiety and avoidance.)

> *"Social anxiety wrapped tighter the longer I stayed. Walking out of my apartment felt like stepping onto a stage. Every glance felt like judgment. I walked the entire perimeter of the building just to enter through the back, avoiding the front entrance. I took tasks in the back whenever I could. Customer interaction drained me. Fluorescent lights buzzed like they were inside my skull."*

> *"When I wasn't working, I disappeared. I avoided stores unless it was an emergency. If Love's didn't stock what I needed, I paid someone else to go to Walmart. When I did go myself, it was at two or three in the morning. Less people. Less threat."*

The work wasn't healing him. It was isolating him. He was surrounded by people – coworkers, customers – and completely alone. The work was the cage he built to hide from the world.

Now here's the realization that came after the crash.

(Read from Chapter 7, page 20 – the realization.)

> *"The move north didn't extinguish the fire. It isolated it. I was surrounded by people and completely alone. Sixteen-hour days and solitary closing shifts didn't silence grief or heal loss. They just proved what I'd been running from my whole life. The storm wasn't outside. It was me."*

> *"I traded whiskey for work. Chaos for control. One addiction for another. From the outside, it looked like progress. Hell, even I thought it was progress. I got clean. I didn't get free."*

> *"What people called 'getting my life together' was really obsession dressed up as discipline."*

Let that line sink in. **"Obsession dressed up as discipline."** That's Law 5.

The world will praise you for working yourself to death. They'll call you dedicated. A team player. A machine. But underneath, you're not winning. You're just fighting a different war on a different battlefield. And the enemy is still you.

Now listen to the pattern. This isn't a one-time thing.

(Read from Chapter 7, page 20 – the pattern.)

> *"The pattern never changed: Get the job. Overperform. Get praised. Get overwhelmed. Crack. Get punished. Disappear. Repeat."*

That's the cycle. Get the job. Overperform. Get praised. Get overwhelmed. Crack. Get punished. Disappear. Repeat.

Different jobs. Different cities. Same loop.

Now read this from the 60 Reflections.

(Read from Chapter 12, page 26 – select lines.)

> *"You can't outperform a dysregulated nervous system. You just exhaust it faster."*

> *"Work became my new pipe. Cleaner. Legal. Still an escape."*

> *"Overperforming isn't discipline. It's panic wearing a suit."*

> *"Burnout doesn't announce itself. It just stops showing up one morning."*

> *"I didn't need more motivation. I needed a nervous system that didn't treat a Tuesday like a threat."*

That last one is the whole thing. **"I didn't need more motivation."** Most people think burnout is a motivation problem. "Just try harder. Just push through." But burnout is not a lack of will. It's a lack of fuel. You can't "push through" when the tank is empty. You just stall.

### [48:00 – 58:00] THE MECHANISM (Whiteboard Drawing)

(Walk to whiteboard. Draw large, simple arrows.)

Here's how work becomes the war you can win – until it kills you. Watch.

**Step 1:** Draw a box. Label it **"LIFE OUT OF CONTROL"**

Relationships failing. Grief unprocessed. Legal trouble. Kids gone. Your nervous system is in chaos. You have no power anywhere else.

**Step 2:** Draw an arrow to a second box. Label it **"WORK IS THE ONE PLACE YOU CAN WIN"**

At work, there are rules. Clear expectations. You show up, you do the task, you get praise. For the first time all day, you feel competent. In control.

**Step 3:** Draw an arrow to a third box. Label it **"YOU OVERPERFORM. COMPULSIVELY."**

You take every shift. Stay late. Never say no. You become indispensable. The praise feels like oxygen. You tell yourself: "This is who I am."

**Step 4:** Draw an arrow to a fourth box. Label it **"YOU EXHAUST YOUR NERVOUS SYSTEM"**

But the work doesn't heal the grief. It just buries it deeper. Your body starts breaking down. Sleep fractures. You're late. You make mistakes. The shame comes back.

**Step 5:** Draw an arrow to a fifth box. Label it **"CRASH"**

One morning you can't get out of bed. Or you get fired. Or you have a breakdown. The work that was saving you has now destroyed you. Because it was never a solution. It was a postponement.

**Step 6:** Draw a loop arrow from the fifth box back to "LIFE OUT OF CONTROL"

Now you have no job, no money, and the same unresolved pain. So you find another job. Different building. Same cage. The loop starts again.

Point to the loop and say:

This is why high-performers crash. Why workaholics burn out. Why the "miracle employee" is often the one closest to falling apart.

**Work becomes the war you can win. But wars don't heal you. They just give you a different place to bleed.**

### [63:00 – 70:00] THE MIRROR (Silent – No talking)

You don't have to say a word. Just sit. Breathe. And notice.

If you've ever been the first one in and the last one out.

If you've ever worked through lunch, through breaks, through sickness – and felt guilty for taking time off.

If you've ever felt anxious on your days off. Like you should be doing something.

If you've ever been praised for "dedication" while secretly falling apart.

If you've ever used work to avoid going home. To avoid the silence. To avoid yourself.

If you've ever crashed – missed a shift, got fired, burned out – and felt more relief than shame.

If you've ever traded one addiction for another – drugs for work, alcohol for exercise, chaos for control – and crashed anyway.

If you've ever felt like you're running on fumes – and have been for years.

That's not laziness. That's not weakness. That's a nervous system that learned: **Control is safety. Performance is love. Rest is danger.**

That's the mirror. That's the machine showing you why you ran so hard for so long.

### [70:00 – 75:00] THE SHIFT + CLIFFHANGER

Here's your tool for this week. One thing.

When you catch yourself working past empty – skipping meals, skipping sleep, saying yes when you want to scream no – don't just push through. Don't call yourself lazy for wanting to stop.

Just name it. Say it out loud or in your head:

**"This isn't discipline. This is panic wearing a suit."**

That's it. Naming it gives your brain one second of air. One second to ask: *Am I building a life, or just hiding from one?*
