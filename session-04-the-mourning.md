## SESSION 04: THE MOURNING

**Time:** 75 minutes  
**Law:** What you couldn't mourn haunts you.  
**Tool:** *"This isn't rage. This is a funeral I never had."*

---

### [0:00 – 8:00] THE ANCHOR

Listen up.

Yellow light. Red light. You run this room. If you need to leave, you leave. If you need to stand, you stand. You are in charge.

Feet flat on the floor. Hand on chest. Hand on belly. Breathe in for four. Hold. Out for six. Do it again. Good.

Last session: Law 3 – A dangerous shelter is still a shelter.

Today: Law 4. Here it is:

**What you couldn't mourn haunts you.**

You've been called "angry." You've been told you have "anger management issues." You've felt a rage so big it feels like it could swallow the room. But underneath that rage? There's a pile of goodbyes you never got to say. A pile of losses you never got to name.

Anger is just grief with teeth. It's the bodyguard for the pain you weren't allowed to feel.

Let's see how that works.

---

### [8:00 – 48:00] THE EPISODE (Expanded)

We're going to read about the twenty-three names again. But this time, we're looking at what happens when you don't get to say goodbye.

(Open the book. Read from Chapter 18, page 37 – the list of names.)

> *"Sylvie Dean Kraus (1988) – Nicholas D. Williams (1999) – Thomas Paul Williams (2000) – Herbert William Lingar (2001) – Judy Ann Lingar (2003) – Argie Faye Williams (2004) – Ashley Nicole Williams (2005) – Warren Wade Ellett (2007) – Chad Argus Acord (2011) – William Garrett Lingar (2011) – Daniel Lee Stewart (2011) – Jerry Don Williams (2012) – Justin Dean Phillips (2015) – Todd Anthony Lingar (2015) – Marie Elizabeth Lingar (2017) – Gabriel Allen Woods (2018) – Jason Jones (2019) – Paula Faye Pitts (2020) – John Wesley Robinson (2020) – Patricia Lynn Lingar (2021) – Deanna Lynn Teague (2021) – Clint Lee Larrison (2022) – Joseph David Middleton (2024) – Billy Aaron Dean Woods (2025)."*

Twenty-three people. That's not just a statistic. That's a lifetime of funerals. But when you're in survival mode, you don't get to go to funerals. You don't get to sit and cry. You have to keep moving. You have to find the next meal, the next job, the next way to stay alive.

So the grief doesn't go away. It just changes shape. It becomes rage.

(Read from Chapter 12, page 26.)

> *"The body keeps score. The mind keeps receipts. The soul keeps the tab."*

The tab is full of people who are gone. And when the tab gets too high, the system overflows. That overflow is the rage you feel at the grocery store, the rage at the driver who cut you off, the rage at the system that doesn't see you.

It's not about the driver. It's about the twenty-three people you couldn't mourn.

Now listen to what happens when the rage finally breaks.

(Read from Chapter 6, page 18 – after Garrett's death.)

> *"A couple days later, back in Arkansas, grief finally crushed me. Lying beside Trinity, the sobs came hard and uncontrollable, shaking the mattress. 'Shut up,' she said. 'I have work tomorrow.' Something inside me broke that night."*

When he finally tried to have the funeral, he was told to shut up. So he put the grief back in the box. He grew teeth. He became the "angry man" because being the "sad man" was too dangerous.

That's Law 4. The rage is the funeral you're still trying to have.

---

### [48:00 – 58:00] THE MECHANISM (Whiteboard Drawing)

(Walk to whiteboard. Draw large, simple arrows.)

Here's how grief turns into rage. Watch.

**Step 1:** Draw a box. Label it **"LOSS"**

Someone dies. You lose a job. You lose your kids. You lose your sense of safety.

**Step 2:** Draw an arrow to a second box. Label it **"SURVIVAL MODE: NO TIME TO MOURN"**

The alarm is going off (Law 1). You have to keep moving. You can't afford to fall apart. So you "suck it up." You "man up." You bury it.

**Step 3:** Draw an arrow to a third box. Label it **"GRIEF COMPRESSES"**

The grief doesn't disappear. It gets packed down. Tight. Like a spring.

**Step 4:** Draw an arrow to a fourth box. Label it **"RAGE = THE OVERFLOW"**

Something small happens. A car backfires. Someone says the wrong thing. The spring snaps. The rage explodes. It feels powerful. It feels active. It feels better than the crushing weight of the loss.

**Step 5:** Draw an arrow to a fifth box. Label it **"SHAME & PUNISHMENT"**

You get in trouble. You lose relationships. People call you "toxic." You feel like a monster.

**Step 6:** Draw a loop arrow from the fifth box back to "LOSS"

Now you've lost even more. The pile of unmourned things gets bigger. The rage gets louder.

Point to the loop and say:

This is why "anger management" doesn't work for trauma. You're trying to fix the steam when you should be looking at the fire. **The rage is just the steam. The unmourned loss is the fire.**

### [63:00 – 70:00] THE MIRROR (Silent – No talking)

You don't have to say a word. Just sit. Breathe. And notice.

If you've ever been told you have an "anger problem."

If you've ever felt a rage so big it scared you.

If you've ever felt like you're carrying a graveyard inside you.

If you've ever felt like crying but the tears wouldn't come – so you yelled instead.

If you've ever felt "numb" to death.

If you've ever felt like the smallest thing could set you off.

If you've ever felt ashamed of your temper.

That's not because you're a bad person. It's because you're a person who has lost too much and been allowed to mourn too little.

That's the mirror. That's the machine showing you the funeral you're still carrying.

---

### [70:00 – 75:00] THE SHIFT + CLIFFHANGER

Here's your tool for this week. One thing.

The next time the rage hits – the heat in your chest, the clenching fists, the urge to scream – don't just call it anger. Don't just try to "calm down."

Just name it. Say it out loud or in your head:

**"This isn't rage. This is a funeral I never had."**

That's it. You're not trying to stop being angry. You're just identifying the source. Naming it gives your brain one second of air. One second to realize that you're not a monster – you're just grieving.
